PublicationCrux News
ArticleThe Bendigo mine supporters: Inside their threats of violence, sabotage, bombs and even death
AuthorPeter Newport
Published16 February 2026
PrinciplesPrinciple 1 (Accuracy, Fairness and Balance); Principle 4 (Comment and Fact)

1. Summary

This is a third-party complaint concerning the above article published by Crux News on 16 February 2026. The complaint is brought in the public interest and concerns the article’s accuracy, fairness, and failure to distinguish between factual reporting and editorial commentary.

The article reports on content posted to the Santana Mine Supporters Facebook page, a public group with more than 5,600 members. It names, photographs, and personally characterises the page moderator, Tom Woodward of Queenstown. Mr Woodward was contacted by Crux for comment and his response is included in the article.

The article is published under the “Crux News” banner as a news report. It is not labelled as opinion, analysis, or editorial. While the existence of objectionable posts on a public Facebook page may be a matter of legitimate public interest, the article contains a material accuracy issue in its opening sentence — a claim that the page “is being investigated by police” for which no police source is cited and which the article’s own content contradicts — and fails throughout to maintain the required distinction between factual reporting and the author’s editorial commentary.

2. Principle 1 (Accuracy, Fairness and Balance)

Principle 1 requires that publications should be bound at all times by accuracy, fairness and balance, and should not deliberately mislead or misinform readers by commission or omission.

a) The claim that the page “is being investigated by police”

The opening sentence of the article states: “A Facebook page that appears to encourage, or condone, violence and death threats against people who oppose the planned $4.5 billion Bendigo Mine is being investigated by police.”

This is presented as established fact in the lead paragraph of a news report. It is not hedged or qualified. It tells the reader, in the article’s most prominent position, that a police investigation is underway.

However, the article cites no police spokesperson, no police media statement, and no named police source for this claim. The only factual basis disclosed within the article itself is the later statement that “at least two formal complaints have been lodged with NZ police.”

A member of the public lodging a complaint with police is not the same as police conducting an investigation. A complaint is an action by a member of the public. An investigation is an action by the police — it implies the matter has been assessed, officers have been assigned, and the subject faces active police inquiry. The distinction is material, not semantic.

The page moderator, Mr Woodward, who is named in the article and most likely to be associated with any such investigation, has confirmed he has not been contacted by any police officer or police representative in relation to the page.

The complainant respectfully asks that the NZMC require Crux to provide the factual basis for its claim that the page “is being investigated by police” — specifically, whether Crux has any police source, statement, or confirmation of an active investigation beyond the knowledge that complaints were lodged by members of the public. If no such source exists, the opening sentence of this news report is inaccurate and misleading, and the author has presented his own assumption as an established fact.

b) The headline attributes the actions of individual posters to all mine supporters

The headline reads: “The Bendigo mine supporters: Inside their threats of violence, sabotage, bombs and even death.” This attributes threats of violence, sabotage, bombs, and death to “the Bendigo mine supporters” as a group — encompassing the page moderator and the 5,600+ page members. The article’s own evidence shows these posts were made by individual users. The headline does not distinguish between the actions of specific individuals and the broader community of supporters.

Santana Minerals’ response, published in the article, states that more than 5,600 people joined the page because they want jobs, investment, and a future for the region. The article selects a handful of extreme posts from individual users and presents them, through the headline and framing, as representative of all mine supporters. No ordinary supporter — other than Mr Woodward — is quoted or given a voice to provide the fair voice Principle 1 requires in articles of controversy or disagreement.

c) Unfair treatment of Mr Woodward

The article makes adverse characterisations about Mr Woodward through editorial devices rather than established facts.

The photograph used is a screen grab from a social media video Mr Woodward made at the Cadia gold mine in New South Wales. The caption describes, in detail, a 2018 tailings dam collapse at that mine, including the volume of tailings that escaped, the insurance settlement, and the environmental damage. The article is about a Facebook page. The photograph and caption have no connection to the subject of the article. The selection of this image and the inclusion of the Cadia disaster in Mr Woodward’s photo caption serve no informational purpose — they associate him with environmental catastrophe. Mr Woodward is a mine worker and has also made documentaries about the beauty of the South Island landscape, a fact the article buries at the end of the caption.

Mr Woodward’s response to Crux is included in the article, but it is positioned after the author’s editorial characterisations and a curated sequence of screenshots that have already led the reader to a conclusion. The article provides a right of reply while structuring itself so that Mr Woodward’s reply appears implausible before the reader reaches it.

d) The people named as targets of abuse were not contacted

The article names Sir Graeme Sydney and Sir Sam Neill as subjects of “personal abuse” on the page. Neither appears to have been contacted for comment about whether they feel threatened by the page content or have themselves made police complaints. Their names are used to add gravity to the article’s characterisation, but their own views are absent.

3. Principle 4 (Comment and Fact)

Principle 4 requires that a clear distinction should be drawn between factual information and comment or opinion, that articles essentially consisting of comment or opinion should be clearly presented as such, and that material facts on which an opinion is based should be accurate.

This article is presented as a news report but contains unlabelled editorial commentary embedded throughout.

a) “The page takes social media toxicity to a new level”

This sentence is pure editorial commentary. It is a value judgement by the author comparing the Mine Supporters page to all other social media and declaring it uniquely extreme. It is not attributed to any source, not labelled as opinion, and is stated as fact within a news report.

b) “A Facebook page that appears to encourage, or condone, violence and death threats”

The opening paragraph presents this characterisation as a factual statement within a news report. The phrase “appears to encourage, or condone” is the author’s interpretive judgement — not a finding by police or any authority. This is the type of speculative assertion using hedging language that the NZMC has specifically cautioned Crux about in previous rulings.

c) The photo caption as editorial commentary

The caption beneath Mr Woodward’s photograph describes in detail the Cadia tailings dam collapse — an event at a different mine, in a different country, unrelated to the subject of the article. Captions in a news report should identify and contextualise the photograph. This caption editorialises by introducing adverse material unrelated to the article’s subject in order to characterise Mr Woodward negatively. It is commentary embedded in a caption within a news report.

d) The article is essentially comment presented as news

Taken as a whole, this article is not a balanced news report. It is constructed to lead the reader to a predetermined conclusion about the Mine Supporters page, about Mr Woodward as moderator, and about the mine support community. The screenshots are factual evidence, but the editorial architecture around them — the headline, the opening characterisation, the “toxicity” claim, the photo selection, the caption, and the sequencing of material before Mr Woodward’s response — constitutes sustained commentary that is not labelled as such.

4. Conclusion

The subject matter of this article — objectionable content on a public Facebook page — is of legitimate public interest. However, public interest does not exempt a publication from maintaining accuracy, fairness, and the distinction between fact and opinion.

The article’s opening sentence claims the Mine Supporters page “is being investigated by police.” No police source is cited. The only basis disclosed in the article is that complaints were lodged. Mr Woodward, the named moderator, has not been contacted by police. The complainant asks that Crux be required to substantiate this claim. If it cannot, the opening sentence of this news report is inaccurate and the author has presented an assumption as established fact.

The article attributes the posts of individual users to all mine supporters through its headline, provides no voice to the thousands of ordinary supporters, uses photographic selection and captioning to characterise Mr Woodward adversely, and embeds unlabelled editorial commentary throughout a piece presented as news.

Mr Woodward was contacted for comment and responded in good faith. His response is included in the article. However, the article’s editorial construction works to undermine his reply rather than present it fairly.

The complainant respectfully requests the NZMC uphold this complaint under:

  • Principle 1 (Accuracy, Fairness and Balance): The article states as fact that the Mine Supporters Facebook page “is being investigated by police” without citing any police source. The only basis disclosed is that complaints were lodged, which is materially different from a police investigation. The headline attributes the actions of individual posters to all mine supporters, the article lacks balance by providing no voice to ordinary supporters, and Mr Woodward is treated unfairly through editorial devices including a deliberately selected photograph and caption unrelated to the article’s subject.
  • Principle 4 (Comment and Fact): The article is published as a news report but contains unlabelled editorial commentary throughout, including the characterisation of the page as taking “social media toxicity to a new level,” the speculative assertion that the page “appears to encourage, or condone, violence,” and a photo caption that editorialises by associating Mr Woodward with an environmental disaster at an unrelated mine. The article fails to clearly distinguish between factual reporting and the author’s editorial commentary.
