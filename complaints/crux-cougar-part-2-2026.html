ArticleNew security and cost questions around QLDC’s CCTV contract
AuthorPeter Newport
Published6 March 2026
PrinciplesPrinciple 1 (Accuracy, Fairness and Balance); Principle 4 (Comment and Fact)

1. Summary

This complaint concerns a news article published by Crux News on 6 March 2026 by editor Peter Newport. The article reports on the cost and security of the Queenstown Lakes District Council’s CCTV network, managed under contract by Cougar Security Ltd. It is presented as a news report with no opinion or analysis label.

The article uses a small number of verified facts as scaffolding for a series of unverified inferences — including a link to a major human rights scandal — that together construct an implied picture of QLDC impropriety. The verified facts do not support the conclusions the article leads readers to draw. QLDC and Cougar Security are denied a genuine right of reply to allegations that go well beyond what the evidence establishes.

2. Principle 4 — Comment and Fact

a) The author’s inferences are presented as factual findings in an unlabelled news report

The article’s opening bullet points — its most prominent position — state that QLDC’s declared camera cost “appears to exclude installation, power, networking, recording and consents.” This is the author’s inference, not a verified fact. Crux’s own LGOIMA request (published in the linked prior article) asked QLDC for “the total cost of these cameras — an approximate figure will be acceptable.” QLDC answered that question literally. The article then criticises the answer as incomplete without disclosing that the narrow framing was Crux’s own. Presenting this inference as a finding in an unlabelled news report, without that context, misleads readers about what the evidence actually shows.

b) The $14 million extrapolation is presented as a factual comparator

The article states that comparable councils have spent up to $33,000 per camera, and that “which would equate to $14 million for QLDC’s 424 cameras.” This projection — the author’s arithmetic from a single external comparison — is presented in a headline bullet as though it were a reported figure. No allowance is made for differences in network scope, specification, terrain, or infrastructure. The article provides no basis for the comparison being valid. Presenting the author’s speculative extrapolation alongside sourced figures, in the same register, blurs the line between fact and comment.

c) The article simultaneously implies QLDC overpaid and underpaid without acknowledging the contradiction

This article is part of a series in which Crux argues QLDC has awarded Cougar Security contracts without competitive tender, implying excessive or improper payments. This article then argues the cameras purchased are suspiciously cheap, implying inadequate procurement. The article holds both positions simultaneously — that QLDC is paying too much for Cougar’s services and too little for the cameras — without acknowledging the tension. This contradiction is not a factual finding; it reflects the author’s editorial disposition toward a narrative of QLDC wrongdoing, presented to readers as though it were a coherent set of facts pointing in the same direction.

3. Principle 1 — Accuracy, Fairness and Balance

a) Linking QLDC cameras to a human rights scandal on an unverified identification

The article states that the cameras “appear to be” Hikvision cameras and that the United States “has banned this type of camera” over human rights and security concerns — concerns that are linked to the mass surveillance of Uyghurs in Xinjiang. The article also states that New Zealand Government departments have “partially adopted” the same ban. The concerns about Hikvision are real and well-documented internationally. However, the sole basis for connecting QLDC’s cameras to those concerns is the author’s visual impression formed while standing on a public street. The article does not confirm the brand. It does not seek technical verification. It does not ask Cougar Security or QLDC whether the cameras are Hikvision. Despite this, the article presents the human rights and security implications as applicable to QLDC’s network. The effect on readers is to associate QLDC’s procurement decisions with cameras linked to atrocities, on the basis of an unverified visual inference. This is inaccurate and unfair.

b) QLDC not given a genuine right of reply

The article states that Crux approached CEO Michelle Morss on the morning of 5 March 2026 — one day before publication. She was travelling and referred the inquiry to her communications team. No substantive response was received before the article was published at midday on 6 March 2026. The allegations in the article — procurement failure, cost misrepresentation, security risk, and an implied link to banned surveillance technology — are serious. Publishing without waiting for or obtaining a substantive response, after less than one working day’s notice with the CEO unavailable, does not meet the requirement to give a fair voice to those criticised.

c) Cougar Security given no effective right of reply

Named individuals are photographed, their business premises described negatively, and detailed allegations made about their contracted work — all without any response being obtained. The article notes that an email was sent to the managing director but gives no indication of when it was sent or how long a response window was allowed. No follow-up is reported. Publishing photographs of named private individuals alongside serious, uncontested professional allegations fails the balance requirement of Principle 1.

4. Conclusion

  • Principle 4 (Comment and Fact): The article presents the author’s inferences and a speculative cost extrapolation as factual findings in an unlabelled news report, and pursues two contradictory lines of criticism without acknowledging the inconsistency, reflecting editorial disposition rather than factual reporting.
  • Principle 1 (Accuracy, Fairness and Balance): The article links QLDC’s cameras to a human rights scandal on the basis of an unverified visual identification; QLDC received less than one working day to respond to serious allegations before publication; and Cougar Security’s named directors were photographed and their work criticised without any response being obtained.
