PrinciplesPrinciple 4 (Comment and Fact); Principle 1 (Accuracy, Fairness and Balance)

1. Summary

This complaint concerns a news article published by Crux News on 4 March 2026, authored by editor Peter Newport, reporting on QLDC's responses to LGOIMA requests about Cougar Security contracts and CCTV cameras.

The article's underlying factual reporting — that QLDC refused certain questions, that an Ombudsman investigation was opened, and that CCTV camera cost information was eventually released — is substantially accurate and well-documented. The complaint does not challenge those facts.

The complaint instead focuses on two discrete but significant failures. First, the article presents Newport's editorial conclusions about QLDC's conduct and motivations as if they were established facts, without labelling, in a publication that carries no indication it is anything other than straight news. Second, the article makes a specific and inaccurate characterisation — that 447 CCTV cameras were "bought using ratepayer funds but given to Cougar" — which is not supported by the source documents Crux itself published, and for which QLDC was given no right of reply.

2. Principle 4 — Comment and Fact

a) Editorial conclusions delivered as news facts without labelling

The article carries no "Opinion," "Analysis," or "Editorial" label. It is presented throughout as a straight news report. Yet it contains editorial conclusions embedded within the factual narrative that cross the line from reporting into advocacy:

  • "The reason for refusing? Basically — it's too hard according to the QLDC" — This is Newport's sarcastic editorial gloss on a lawful statutory response. The word "basically" and the mocking framing signal opinion, yet no label signals this to readers.
  • "Even though the QLDC said in December this information 'did not exist' the Ombudsman's investigator has persuaded the council to 'find' the costs" — The use of scare quotes around "find" implies deliberate deception or incompetence by QLDC. This is a conclusion, not a fact. Whether QLDC originally had this information and withheld it, or genuinely could not produce it until a more targeted question was asked, remains contested and unresolved. Presenting the more damaging interpretation as fact, without labelling it as Newport's opinion, breaches Principle 4.
  • "What we don't yet know is why the council purchased these cameras so that they could be handed over to Cougar and then paid ongoing fees to Cougar to operate them" — This sentence implies a suspicious or irregular arrangement as an editorial framing device. The premise that cameras were "handed over" to Cougar is Newport's characterisation, not an established finding. Cougar is contracted to operate a network owned and controlled by QLDC — that is what the source documents show. Presenting this as a mysterious unanswered question, within a news article, without labelling, is opinion framed as fact.

These examples mirror the pattern the Council criticised in Case 2939, where it found that Newport "needs to be careful in his blurring of the lines between fact and opinion" and that the article was "littered with wording" that presented the journalist's own conclusions as though they were evidenced findings.

b) Newport as journalist and protagonist — unlabelled advocacy

The article reports on Crux's own LGOIMA requests, Crux's own decision to decline QLDC's invitation to narrow its questions, and Crux's own Ombudsman complaint. Newport is simultaneously the reporter and a central actor in the story. This is a form of advocacy journalism that the Council has consistently noted requires clear labelling. In Case 2939 the Council observed that Newport "employs an unusual style of reportage… a type of advocacy journalism, but he needs to be careful in his blurring of the lines between fact and opinion." In Case 2891 the Council noted that "readers will be perplexed as to whether this article is a work of fact or comment." That perplexity is present here: a reader cannot tell whether they are reading a neutral account of events or Crux's advocacy for its own position in an ongoing dispute with QLDC.

3. Principle 1 — Accuracy, Fairness and Balance

a) The "handed over to Cougar" characterisation is inaccurate and unchallenged

The article states, in its opening paragraphs, that these are "447 CCTV cameras (bought using ratepayer funds but given to Cougar)" and later refers to "cameras that have been purchased by QLDC, but then given to Cougar Security Ltd to operate."

The QLDC LGOIMA response of December 19, 2025 — published by Crux as a source document — states clearly: "Cougar Group is contracted to maintain QLDC's CCTV network and operates the centralised system within their secure premises. They have system-level access to support maintenance and network management." It further states: "No cameras have been installed at the direct request of Cougar. All installations are initiated by Council in response to an identified incident or business requirement."

The cameras were not "given to Cougar." They are QLDC-owned assets operated by a contractor under contract. The description "given to Cougar" is inaccurate. It implies a transfer of ownership or control that the source documents do not support and that QLDC explicitly contradicts. QLDC was not given an opportunity to respond to this specific characterisation within the article. This is both an accuracy failure and a balance failure, directly analogous to the finding in Case 3816 that it was "unfair and unbalanced not to give [those criticised] an opportunity to comment" on specific claims made about them.

b) No right of reply to editorial characterisations of QLDC's motives

The article repeatedly implies that QLDC's refusals are motivated by a desire to conceal impropriety — the "basically too hard" framing, the scare-quoted "find," the mysterious "why did they hand cameras to Cougar" conclusion. These are specific editorial imputations of bad faith. QLDC's written LGOIMA responses are included as linked documents, but that is not equivalent to giving QLDC a right of reply to the editorial narrative Newport has constructed around those documents.

The Council found in Case 3834 that Crux failed to meet balance requirements by not giving the subject an opportunity to respond to specific characterisations. The principle applies here with equal force: a subject who is characterised as acting in bad faith is entitled to an opportunity to respond to that characterisation in the article itself, not merely to have their official correspondence attached as a PDF.

c) The "community interest" framing is asserted without evidence

The article opens by stating Crux asked these questions "responding to widespread community interest." No evidence is provided for this claim. The framing implies broad public demand for Crux's investigation, lending the article an air of community mandate it has not established. This is a minor but illustrative example of assertion presented as fact — consistent with the pattern the Council has noted across Crux's history.

4. Conclusion

The underlying public interest journalism in this article — scrutinising a major council contractor's procurement and contract management — is legitimate. The factual core of the reporting is sound. This complaint does not dispute those elements.

The breaches arise from specific and identifiable failures: an inaccurate characterisation of the camera arrangements as cameras "given to Cougar"; editorial conclusions about QLDC's motives and conduct presented as news facts without labelling; and the absence of right of reply to those specific characterisations. These are the same failures the Council has repeatedly identified in Crux's work across nine complaints spanning six years.

The complainant respectfully submits that the article breaches:

  • Principle 4 (Comment and Fact) — by presenting editorial conclusions as news facts without labelling
  • Principle 1 (Accuracy, Fairness and Balance) — by inaccurately characterising the camera arrangements and failing to provide right of reply to specific editorial characterisations
