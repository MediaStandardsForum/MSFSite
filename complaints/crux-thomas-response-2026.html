ArticleRevealed: The QLDC conversation Skyline's Richard Thomas tried to hide
AuthorPeter Newport
Published16 February 2026
PrinciplesPrinciple 1 (Accuracy, Fairness and Balance); Principle 4 (Comment and Fact)

1. Summary

This complaint concerns two articles published by Crux about Queenstown businessman Richard Thomas, both by Peter Newport. Article 1 was published on 29 January 2026 while Mr Thomas’s earlier complaint (Case 3834) was pending before the Council. Article 2 was published on 16 February 2026 — the same day the Council published its ruling in Case 3834 upholding Mr Thomas’s complaint under both Principles (1) and (4).

Both articles repeat the identical conduct the Council found to be in breach in Case 3834: presenting unsupported assertions as established facts, failing to distinguish between comment and fact, and treating Mr Thomas unfairly. Together they form a continuing pattern of the conduct the Media Council has identified across ten previous cases involving Crux (Cases 2891, 2895, 2939, 2940, 3012, 3338, 3356, 3804, 3816, and 3834): the blurring of fact and opinion, the presentation of assertion as established fact, and the failure to provide fair balance.

2. Article 1 — “Revealed: The QLDC conversation Skyline’s Richard Thomas tried to hide” (29 January 2026)

Principle 4 — Comment and Fact

a) The headline imputes dishonesty as fact

The headline states Mr Thomas “tried to hide” a conversation. The word “hide” directly imputes dishonest concealment. No evidence of concealment is presented in the article. Mr Thomas sought an affidavit from Mayor Glover to clarify the factual record — seeking to clarify through a legal instrument is not an act of concealment. As the Council found in Case 3804 (paragraph 25): “The label cannot be used to excuse unsupported claims or personal assertions that cannot be substantiated.”

b) The article’s own source does not support its conclusions

Councillor Mitchell — Crux’s primary source — states he recalled Mr Thomas “suggested” Councillor Copland “would be a good representative on the regional deal joint committee, on the grounds he is an accountant.” Mitchell further states: “I don’t recall if Richard was suggesting John Glover (mayor) or Quentin Smith (deputy mayor) weren’t right for the role.” Despite this measured account, the article asserts Mr Thomas “crossed a line at the function by suggesting a ‘favoured alternate’ be put on the Regional Deals committee, replacing the Deputy Mayor or the Mayor.” This goes substantially beyond what Mitchell said. As the Council found in Case 2939: “Assertion is not fact and while any journalist can report assertions of a source, Newport in these news stories is the journalist, not the source.”

c) Speculative assertions presented as fact

The article states the affidavit request “suggests Mr Thomas knows he has crossed an important line” — a conclusion about Mr Thomas’s state of mind presented as factual observation. It introduces unsubstantiated claims about unnamed National MPs expressing “displeasure” about Mayor Glover. It characterises the business community as having a “tarnished ‘old school elite boys club’ reputation.” None of these are attributed or evidenced.

d) Published while Case 3834 was pending

The article extends the same allegations that were under Council review, linking directly to the article subject to Mr Thomas’s complaint without acknowledging it was under complaint.

Principle 1 — Accuracy, Fairness and Balance

a) Inadequate right of reply

The article makes serious allegations about Mr Thomas — accusing him of trying to hide a conversation, crossing an important line, and attempting to undermine the democratic process. While Mr Thomas was contacted by Crux, he declined to respond. The article notes he “refused to respond to Crux emails and texts.” However, this correspondence related to prior matters and did not put to Mr Thomas the specific allegations and characterisations contained in this article — including the headline claim that he “tried to hide” a conversation, the assertion that he “crossed a line,” or the claim that the affidavit request demonstrated he knew he had acted improperly. General correspondence does not satisfy the obligation to provide a right of reply when new and serious allegations are being made. This is consistent with the Council’s finding in Case 3834, where the complaint was upheld in part because Mr Thomas was not given adequate opportunity to comment.

b) Material omissions

The article does not disclose that Crux’s original reporting incorrectly stated the conversation was with Mayor Glover. Rather than framing this as a correction, Article 1 presents the updated information as a new revelation strengthening Crux’s narrative.

c) Unfair tone

Mr Thomas is described as “Skyline heir,” accused of hiding conversations and crossing lines, and characterised as “secretive and sensitive.” This mirrors the “sarcastic and derogatory” tone the Council identified in Case 3804 (paragraph 17).

3. Article 2 — “Response from Crux Editor (and comment from Richard Thomas) after Media Council complaint” (16 February 2026)

Principle 4 — Comment and Fact

a) Unsubstantiated claims in editorial framing

The article states Crux received “credible information from multiple sources” that Mr Thomas “tried to undermine” the Regional Deals committee. No sources are named, no evidence is provided, and “undermine” is presented as fact rather than interpretation.

b) Questions presuppose unproven facts

Five questions directed at Mr Thomas embed unproven claims: presupposing his involvement in election campaigns “going back ten years,” presupposing he “sought to undermine a decision already made by elected members,” and presupposing a connection to “Scope Communication.”

The question about “Scope Communication” and whether Mr Thomas was “the client and invoiced party” for election campaigns is not merely an unsubstantiated presupposition — it is false. Mr Thomas has never paid Scope Communications for any election campaign. This factual claim, embedded within a question, is directly contradicted by evidence Crux itself has published. In its own article “Mayor Lewers reveals $10,000 election donation from Rod Drury,” Crux published Mayor Lewers’ statement that “Richard Thomas is a trusted friend and colleague. Richard has made no financial contribution to my campaign.” Mayor Lewers further stated that he personally engaged Scope Communications and that all paid providers were operating within their allocated budgets. Crux therefore had published evidence in its own reporting that contradicted the premise of the question it put to Mr Thomas, yet presented that premise to readers as though it were an established possibility requiring confirmation or denial. These are not genuine questions seeking information — they are assertions framed as questions. This achieves the same outcome the Council criticised in Case 3834 at paragraph 37: “The statements are conclusory and no evidence is supplied.” It is also a clear example of what the Council described in Case 2940: “Crux has misrepresented opinion as material facts.”

c) Article 1 mischaracterised as “confirmation”

Article 2 states Crux has “subsequently published confirmation” of the DQ conversation. As set out in Section 2, Councillor Mitchell’s actual account does not confirm Crux’s characterisation. His measured recollection of a suggestion is presented as confirmation of an attempt to undermine — blurring fact and interpretation.

d) The claim Crux never accused Mr Thomas of dishonesty is contradicted by its own material

Article 2 asserts: “Crux has at no time accused Mr Thomas of dishonesty.” Article 1’s headline states Mr Thomas “tried to hide” a conversation. Article 1 describes him as “so fervent in suggesting that this conversation never took place” and asks “Why is he so secretive and sensitive?” Mr Thomas’s quoted response in Article 2 — “You accuse me of dishonesty” — reflects how Crux’s coverage plainly reads. The assertion that Crux has never made such an accusation is a material factual claim contradicted by Crux’s own published words.

Principle 1 — Accuracy, Fairness and Balance

a) Unfair presentation of Mr Thomas’s response

Mr Thomas’s emailed reply is published with the editorial comment that it “failed to address any of the questions.” His reasons for declining — that he considers Crux’s coverage defamatory and the questions an attempt to further ill-founded allegations — are presented as evasiveness rather than a legitimate position. The Council has now upheld his complaint in Case 3834, validating the concerns he expressed. His refusal does not relieve Crux of its obligations. As the Council found in Case 3834 (paragraph 38): “It should be obvious to Crux that if they want to report claims of wrongdoing at specific meetings, they should do the investigative journalism required to establish the facts.”

b) The Council’s findings are omitted

Article 2 summarises the Case 3834 ruling narrowly, omitting the Council’s findings that there was “no actual analysis of facts or principles,” that statements were “conclusory and no evidence is supplied,” and that continued findings bring into question Crux’s ability to maintain standards. A reader would have no idea of the strength of the Council’s criticism.

c) The ruling is relitigated on the day it is published

Rather than accepting the ruling, Article 2 introduces new unsubstantiated claims that re-prosecute the same allegations the Council has just found to be in breach.

4. Crux’s Inability to Meet Required Standards

In Case 3834 (paragraph 40), the Council stated: “Continued findings against Crux brings into question its ability to meet and maintain the required high standards.”

The Council’s own rulings trace a six-year pattern of identical violations:

Case 2891 (2020, upheld): “a collision of both without clear labelling.” Case 2895 (2020, cautioned): “should give Crux pause for thought.” Case 2939 (2020, partially upheld): “assertion is not fact.” Case 2940 (2020, upheld): “Crux has misrepresented opinion as material facts.” Case 3012 (2021, criticised): “Crux’s reporting has repeatedly crossed the line.” Case 3356 (2022, upheld): “disappointed by Crux’s conduct.” Case 3804 (2025, upheld): “fallen well short of meeting the ‘highest professional standards’.” Case 3816 (2025, upheld on four articles): multiple breaches of Principle 4. Case 3834 (2026, upheld): “brings into question its ability to meet and maintain the required high standards.”

At each stage, the Council’s warnings have grown stronger. Crux’s conduct has not changed. The articles under complaint — one published while Case 3834 was pending, the other on the day of the ruling — demonstrate that warnings and upheld complaints have no deterrent effect.

Conclusion

  • Principle 4 (Comment and Fact): Both articles present editorial assertions and speculation as established facts, embed unproven claims within questions, and mischaracterise evidence in a manner that fails to draw a clear distinction between factual information and comment or opinion.
  • Principle 1 (Accuracy, Fairness and Balance): Both articles treat Mr Thomas unfairly by presenting his positions in a misleading light, omitting material context including the substance of the Council’s own findings, and relitigating upheld complaints through fresh unsubstantiated allegations.

5. Relief Sought

This complaint is made under Principle 4 (Comment and Fact) and Principle 1 (Accuracy, Fairness and Balance) in respect of both articles. Corrections are requested.

The Council’s attention is drawn to Rule 25 of the Complaints Procedure, which provides for censure “in egregious circumstances, with a unanimous decision.” The Council declined censure in Case 3804, finding the threshold was not met. That decision preceded Cases 3816 and 3834, and preceded Crux’s response to those findings — publishing Article 1 while 3834 was pending and Article 2 on the day of the ruling.

Individual complaints have proven insufficient. Warnings of increasing severity have been disregarded. The Council is respectfully asked to consider whether the cumulative record meets the threshold for censure. If it does not, the Council is asked to consider what further steps might achieve the compliance that ten previous cases have failed to produce.
