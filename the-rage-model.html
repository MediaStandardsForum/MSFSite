Media Standards Forum

The Rage Model

How outrage-driven journalism, financial dependency, and undeclared interests converge at Crux News

For six years, Peter Newport's position was clear. Council advertising compromises journalism. You cannot hold power to account while depending on power for revenue. "You simply can't bite the hand that feeds you," he wrote. Crux would never take council money.

By 2026, he was demanding his "fair share" of the QLDC advertising budget.

Between those two positions lies a financial crisis, a local body election, and a pattern of coverage so extreme that the New Zealand Media Council questioned whether Crux could meet the standards required of journalism. The question is not whether Newport changed his mind. The question is what changed it — and what the public lost in the process.

The Principle

Crux launched in Queenstown in 2018 with an explicit editorial firewall. Council notices could appear in a community bulletin. They would never appear in the main news feed. Crux received no advertising or other funding from local councils.

Newport accused competitors like the Mountain Scene of being editorially captured by council advertising. Crux would be different. Independent. Unbuyable.

Government support through the Public Interest Journalism Fund helped sustain the model. The PIJF provided $55 million across the NZ media sector between 2021 and 2023. Crux was a direct beneficiary. When the fund ended and the incoming National-led government declined to replace it, Crux had no fallback.

In November 2024, Newport announced hibernation. Staff were made redundant. Assets were sold. A subscriber surge allowed a partial relaunch on Substack — but the operation that emerged could fund only one person, Newport himself, at around half a market-level salary. Less than 15% of Substack subscribers were paying.

The audience, however, remained enormous by local standards: one million monthly Facebook views, 45,000 unique readers, 400,000 page views. Facebook was not just a distribution channel. It was the business.

The Flip

By mid-2025, the principle was gone.

Crux began publishing OIA data on QLDC advertising expenditure, framing the $200,000 flowing to Allied Press as a subsidy that Crux was entitled to share. Newport presented QLDC's interim CEO with a formal media pack. The council's comms team replied that they would not consider advertising with Crux.

Newport's response was public and direct: "Crux requests this year for a fair share of that QLDC advertising budget have so far been 100% ignored."

He lobbied Media Minister Paul Goldsmith. He framed the entire question as one of fairness and transparency.

In the same article, he wrote that advertising "was never a natural fit for news" and that "locally you can't bite the hand that feeds you."

The contradiction sat in the same paragraph.

The Outrage Dividend

Crux's editorial focus is QLDC accountability — inherently adversarial, inherently negative. This is not a criticism. It is a description of the content that generates the most engagement on the platform Crux depends on for survival.

Research at Yale, published in Science Advances, found that social media platforms systematically amplify moral outrage. Users who receive more engagement for expressing outrage learn to express more outrage — a reinforcement loop. Facebook's own internal research, leaked by whistleblower Frances Haugen, confirmed the same pattern: the platform's algorithm rewarded outrage and incentivised sensationalism.

For a publisher whose primary distribution channel is Facebook and whose financial survival depends on audience growth, the structural incentive is to produce content that makes people angry. The angrier the content, the wider the reach. The wider the reach, the stronger the case for advertising revenue.

Newport acknowledged the dynamic. "We are certainly guilty of letting our frustration come through in our coverage on occasion," he wrote. "I'm from a strong commercial news background and that does involve headlines that are strong and encourage people to read our stories."

This is a description of a business model, not a confession.

The Election

The 2025 QLDC local body elections closed on 11 October. John Glover defeated incumbent mayor Glyn Lewers. Much of the council changed.

Crux's coverage was not neutral and did not attempt to be.

The publication ran its own election polls, consistently showing Lewers in a distant fourth. It gave Councillor Niki Gladding — who had become Crux's primary council source — a direct editorial platform, publishing articles under her name and her full statements critical of Lewers. It systematically targeted the Lewers campaign team by name: identifying his PR consultants, his business backers, and even a QLDC communications staffer who had personally liked Lewers' social media posts.

On 4 December 2025, less than two months after the election, Crux published the article that became NZMC Case 3838. It argued that businessman Richard Thomas had "groomed" Lewers for the mayoralty, influenced councillors "from the shadows," and run an "aggressive, sometimes abusive" campaign. Thomas was not offered a right of reply.

The Media Council upheld the complaint — accuracy, fairness, balance, and the distinction between comment and fact. The Council observed that Crux had "a number of complaints upheld against it involving extreme statements of opinion about local politicians" and that continued findings "brings into question its ability to meet and maintain the required high standards." It was not the only election-period finding. A separate complaint covering multiple Crux articles from the same campaign was also partially upheld on the same grounds.

The Undeclared Interest

Here is the question that has not been asked.

During the 2025 election, Newport was pivoting Crux's entire business model toward council advertising. The composition of the council — which candidates held seats — would directly determine whether Crux received that revenue.

Newport framed the QLDC advertising story as a corruption narrative: council money flowing to the Mountain Scene, propping up a publication with ties to Richard Thomas, who backed Lewers. It is a compelling narrative. But the mirror image is equally compelling. If Newport's preferred candidates were elected and then directed advertising to Crux, it would be exactly the same dynamic he accused his competitors of benefiting from.

Newport did not declare this financial interest to his readers. They were not told that Crux's editorial line on the election — which candidates to support, which to oppose — aligned with a business model that required sympathetic councillors to approve advertising spend.

After the election, Newport wrote that it was "perhaps naive to expect rapid progress in terms of accountability/transparency after the election of a new mayor and many new councillors last October." He then immediately complained that his advertising requests were being ignored. The timeline speaks for itself.

The Model

This is not a conspiracy. It is a structure.

Government funding for journalism ends. A hyper-local publisher, serving 50,000 people, cannot sustain itself through subscriptions alone. The only remaining growth lever is Facebook engagement. Facebook rewards outrage. The publisher's editorial focus is local government accountability — a topic that generates maximum engagement when framed as maximum anger. The business model does not just tolerate rage. It depends on it.

Layer onto that a local election in which the publisher has an undeclared financial interest in the outcome. Layer onto that a media standards body formally finding that the election coverage involved extreme statements of opinion about politicians, presented as fact.

The New Zealand Media Council has said, in its own words, that Crux's conduct brings into question its ability to meet required standards. Peter Newport agreed to those standards when he joined the Council. He cannot claim the credibility of membership while dismissing its findings.

And the people of Queenstown, Wanaka, and Cromwell cannot make informed decisions about their democracy if the publication they rely on for accountability journalism has an undeclared financial interest in who sits at the council table.
